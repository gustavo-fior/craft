You can carbon-date an interface. Skeuomorphic leather: 2011. Long
shadows: 2014. Glassmorphism: 2021. Every aesthetic that arrives as a
wave leaves as one, and it takes your product's credibility with it -
an interface styled by trend announces its own age the way a haircut
does.

Shu Ding's essays keep returning to Dieter Rams: good design is honest,
unobtrusive, long-lasting. Note what's *not* on the list - novel,
current, impressive. Rams designed radios that look contemporary fifty
years later by refusing to design them to look contemporary at all.

## What actually ages well

- **Structure over surface.** Hierarchy, spacing, alignment, and rhythm
  read the same in any decade. The Swiss grid is pushing eighty.
- **Function-shaped decisions.** The Dynamic Island earned its shape - it
  wraps a physical sensor cutout. The imitations that copied the pill onto
  screens without one copied the fashion and left the reason; those aged
  in months.
- **Honest materials.** Text that looks like text, buttons that look
  pressable. Effects that pretend depth or texture the platform doesn't
  have are the first thing to curdle.
- **Restraint.** Almost nothing on a monochrome, type-led interface can
  expire. This is half of why terminal aesthetics keep coming back.

## Not a ban on style

Timelessness isn't beige. It's asking, before adopting anything: would
this decision still make sense if the trend around it vanished? Borrow
from waves freely - but only the parts you can justify without pointing
at what everyone else shipped this quarter. Trends are a reason to
examine a technique, never a reason to use one.
